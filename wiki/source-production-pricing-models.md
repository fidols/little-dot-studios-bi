# Source: Content Production Pricing Models

**Sources:** columnfivemedia.com, wearecovalent.com, bettervideocontent.com
**Ingested:** 2026-05-12

## The Three Main Pricing Models

### 1. Retainer
- Client pays a fixed monthly fee for ongoing content production/management
- **Best for:** Channel management, social media, Content ID — recurring deliverables
- **Advantages:** Predictable revenue for LDS, easier staffing planning
- **BI implication:** Profitability tracked as margin per retainer. Watch for scope creep eating into margin.

### 2. Project-Based (Fixed Fee)
- Client pays a flat fee for a defined deliverable (e.g., a campaign, a video series)
- **Best for:** WING productions — McLaren campaigns, Olympics coverage
- **Risk:** Underestimate hours and the project loses money
- **BI implication:** Budget vs. actual variance is the key metric. Historical actuals inform future estimates.

### 3. Hourly / Day Rate
- Client billed for actual hours at an agreed rate
- **Best for:** Ad hoc work, consultancy, overflow production
- **Advantages:** No underestimation risk
- **Disadvantage:** Clients prefer fixed fees; harder to sell

| Type | Cost Range |
|---|---|
| Social content video | $1,500–$5,000 |
| Corporate explainer | $4,500–$20,000 |

## Budget Allocation by Phase
- **Pre-production:** 15–25% of total budget
- **Production:** 40–55%
- **Post-production:** 25–35%

## How Pricing Relates to the BI Role
The JD says: "Serve as the pricing point of contact for new business and renewal pitches, collaborating with Finance and Commercial teams on staffing assumptions and delivery models."

In practice this means:
1. A new client comes in — Sales brings it to Commercial Planning
2. BI Analyst pulls historical actuals: "A project like this took X hours last time, here's the role breakdown"
3. Finance applies loaded cost rates to calculate the floor price
4. Commercial sets the margin target
5. BI Analyst builds the staffing model: how many people, what roles, how many days
6. Output: a pricing proposal with confident assumptions backed by data
